Just to hear homophobia rather than see it behind a screen is so much more impactful. 

(2/2) “Two days after the election result, my school had a walkout. This guy comes in complaining about the walkout. Because they were considered an acceptance because of sexual arousal. My school is generally liberal, but there is still so much discrimination occurring. I’ve always grew up in a nice middle class cultured family but I didn’t know how it was like to live at the bottom until I was homeless.

(3/4) “My friends and I growing up had lots of pains. Everyone had their own pains so we were self-medicating with marijuana and drugs. My mother wasn’t having it, and I was sent out on my own when I was 19. I was homeless for half a year. It’s that where you feel as though in Ralph Ellis’s book that you were an invisible person. People just look past you. They don’t look at you. They look past you like you didn’t exist.”

“How did you pick yourself back up?”

“When I was giving up on myself, my grandmother and aunts were praying for me. They had faith that God would protect and save me, which he did. Once you hit bottom, you can only go up. My father eventually picked me up. We went to the acorn projects in West Oakland, and I got my degree on the streets. I’ve always grew up in a nice middle class cultured family but I didn’t know how it was like to live at the bottom until I was homeless and lived in the acorn projects. It opened my eyes, and I became tough.
Some of these people at school think they’re tough, think they’re gangsters. But you have no idea what being tough is until you live in a community where women are being raped, drug deals are a common place, people are being ripped off, violence and crime is daily experience. The police take their time coming or don’t come at all. You live on food stamps or donations on the church. You’re treated as though you’re less than human, which was how I was treated when I went to social services. People don’t choose to be poor, but it’s because of the way society is set.”

“What’s one piece of advice you’ve acquired over the years and would share with the world?”

“Learn to forgive and let go. Forgive, love, and let it go. Don’t carry it. As I studied all the other faiths, I came with the same understanding that the key to live in this world is to love others, love God if you have the faith, and love yourself..“

Until one day I was at Christopher Ho’s house and his mother said in Cantonese, 'You’re not supposed to be with this black guy.' 

(2/4) “Every summer, my mother would ship us back to New Orleans so we would not lose contact with our roots and family. I experienced what it was like to be under Jim Crow. I remember African Americans being lynched and burned. I remember it like it was yesterday. I was young at the time but I remember it on the news. It would break my heart when we’re supposed to be in a place called America, the beautiful and justice for all. I didn’t see that growing up. In elementary school, I had Christopher Ho who was Chinese, Hiro Nagafushi who was Japanese, Byron and Bruce who were African American, and Richard who was white. We had the biggest gang at Washington Elementary School. We biked everywhere, went everywhere together, and did everything. I thought that was the way everyone was supposed to live. Until one day I was at Christopher Ho’s house and his mother said in Cantonese, You’re not supposed to be with this black guy.' He teared up in the kitchen of his house because we were friends. This was in fourth grade. He went outside and told me,' You can’t come to my house anymore.' I’ll never forget that day. But, the beauty of it all is that we’re all still friends. One of my biggest struggle growing up in a society like that was deprogramming myself from the hypocrisy of the media and the propaganda of thinking that I’m less of a human being because I’m not born white. Because of that experience, I’ve never felt that anybody should feel superior to others, that no one should be left down because of their culture, race, or sex . I’m against all the -isms because I grew up in that environment and I had great teachers and great schools. I learned that even though we have progressed with an African American president, I see pockets of these situations where we were like back in the 1950s.“

My generation brought us a little further but this generation should take us even further.

(1/4) “I was born in the South. My parents moved me and my sister here to get away from Jim Crow and all the racism and prejudice of the South. The most influential person to me when she was living was my grandma. She was my nanny. She always doted on me so when I was little and treated me like I was older. When I was five years old, we walked to the bank, and she opened a bank account for me. She bought me my first piggybank. From that day forward, I had a business mind. I counted all the money when the big day came to break my piggybank, wrote down all the figures, and went to the bank. I knew how to collect stamps. When no one would go to church and partied every Saturday and Sunday night instead, she would take me to church on Sunday morning. She taught me the history of my family, that I came from a line of educators where my great grandfather said it was a means of empowerment and liberations. She had a high expectation of me and stressed the importance of giving back.
I subbed at a juvenile hall and alternative middle school that was named after President Obama. I remember working with young kids that felt alienated and a stigma attached to being part of an alternative school. They organized, worked hard, and presented to a school board. They had faith, the hope, and the confidence in themselves. They were really proud. The board and even Congresswoman Barbara Lee were there for the dedication. It was a great experience for those kids to see that they can make a difference.
I started off as a youth pastor then went to Florida where I worked with the school system to address the issue of why so many kids were failing and doing what they’re not supposed to do. All the schools were failing and not doing so well, at least students of color. I grew disenchanted with the fact that kids were continuing to fail, continuing to drop out, and I thought we weren’t going in the right direction. I found many of the answers in the charter school system. I worked long hours but it’s not about the money. It’s about helping shape young souls, young lives. We are impacting the future. My generation brought us a little further but this generation should take us even further.”
