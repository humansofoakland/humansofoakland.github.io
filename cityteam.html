 I promised my mom a lot of things, but in high school, things changed. 

(⅓) “I grew up in a town called King City. I was raised by a single mother. I don’t know my father. It doesn’t bug me at all, but I think it has something to do with why I’m here. I’m the youngest of four boys. My brother gang banged, and I saw my mother cry a lot. I saw her pain. I used to be really bad. I was the bully and picked fights. When I was in sixth grade, I realized what was going on and changed. I started to do better in school. By the end of middle school, I was in the top ten percent of my class. My mom converted to Christianity, and I got baptized. I promised my mom a lot of things, but in high school, things changed. I was a victim of peer pressure and wanting to always fit in. I tried cocaine for the first time when I was in eighth grade, but I didn’t like the feeling. So I started smoking marijuana. By tenth grade, I moved to the west side of my town and got introduced to crystal meth and left marijuana. I started hearing voices and didn’t trust nobody so when I was 16, I asked for help. I ended up moving to Union City for 6 months and moved in with a pastor. After six months, I thought I was ready to move back to my town, but I just started getting back to the same things. In the summer of 2006 when my brother got married, my cousin heard what was happening to me and asked my mother if she could take me back to Sacramento. So I moved to Sacramento and graduated high school, but I was still using. I started experimenting with ecstasy, but I eventually went back to crystal meth. In 2011, I was 22 at the time. I got incarcerated for the first time, and I went to prison. I was selling methylamine and ended up getting set up by the buyer. I got caught with a quarter pound, and I was sentenced to three years with halftime. After I got out, I didn’t get in trouble the whole year. I thought I was done with that life. I moved back to my hometown and started seeing old friends. But little by little, I started selling again and it went on for three years.”

I tried. I really did, but it’s an addiction some of us can’t. We’re powerless.

(⅔) “On Thanksgiving of 2014, I received a phone call from my ex-girlfriend. We hadn’t seen or talked to each other for a whole month. That morning, she told me she was pregnant. I asked her how she knew. She said it was the second test and it had come up positive. She later moved in with me. She was already seven months pregnant. My daughter, Camila, was born July 21, 2015. A month later, I ended up telling her to leave because she was physically abusing me. Hitting a girl is not in my genes. I was raised by a single mother and I would never hit a woman. It started off as verbal, talking smack to me. Eventually, it got physical. But at the same time, I was using methylamine and trying to numb out my feelings. My daughter was old enough to remember you if she saw you two days ago. I couldn’t let her see her mother beat me up. So, I told her mother to go back home, and she gained a big resentment. She wouldn’t let me see my daughter until I move out of King City and stopped selling drugs. So, I stopped selling but the addiction still continued. I couldn’t stop using. On October 12, I got incarcerated again because I had failed to appear at a probation meeting. Two days before my court date, I asked them for a program because no matter what I tried, I couldn’t see my daughter. Not only that, but I wasn’t providing for her or my daughter. I needed to get my life straight because with no job, no money in my pocket, I somehow managed to still be using. I realized that was wrong and as a man with a kid, I wasn’t doing what I was supposed to be doing. I grew up without a dad. I didn’t want my daughter growing up without her father. That little girl needs her dad. And I need her. Not being with her right now hurts me. She’s young. She’s only fifteen months. She probably won’t remember me. My ex blames me and my addiction. She says I’m weak because I need a program to help me with my addiction. I tried, I really did, but it’s an addiction some of us can’t. We’re powerless. Everyone’s wired differently.”

I came here primarily for my daughter, but since coming here, I began to see myself in a new light. 

(3/3) “On October 23, they picked me up and brought me here to Cityteam. I don’t know what the Lord has in store for me, but this whole month I’ve been here has been great. I’ve been blessed. I have 36 days sober. I’m not counting but it’s something I cherish. They say here, ‘Don’t count the days, make the days count.’ That’s what I’m trying to do everyday. It’s a process of humbling myself through helping feed the community everyday, working. In the program, people pointed out I have a positive attitude. I never thought I did because I was always high. I never really knew myself. I came here primarily for my daughter, but since coming here, I began to see myself in a new light. God has blessed me. I’m leaving it up to God. There’s a scripture in the Bible that says, ‘Take delight in the Lord and he will give you what your heart desires.’ I’m really standing by that verse and hopeful I can get not my whole family back, but at least my daughter.”

No one knew the consequences back then. 

(1/2) “I was born and raised in Philadelphia. I’m 53 years old so I grew up in a generation where everyone thought it was cool to get high. No one knew the consequences back then. Everyone wanted to have fun and go out to parties. I didn’t want to stop. I went to all the clubs when I was in my twenties. Thought it was cool. It later dragged on until I was addicted. It became a habit and I wanted it all the time. It didn’t really cause problems until I was in my forties. I’ve never been to prison. I’ve walked all my life, but in my forties, it just wasn’t working anymore. I didn’t take it seriously that I had a problem until maybe five years ago. So I started to try and get help.”

It took all these years to come to a point of how I’m going to live. 

(2/2) “When I first started working at the homeless shelter, I did not have a lot of patience for the men. Then, I started to get to know them and realize they’re just people. I began getting to know their stories ,and it changed my view of who they were. A lot of them are just down in the rocks. Some of them gave up on life. Some had mental issues. It gave me a little more understanding and compassion towards them. I realized I’m no better than them no matter what. I got saved 26 years ago. I tried to live in two worlds: the world and all that it has to offer and church. It took all these years to come to a point of how I’m going to live.  I decided to live my life as a Christian. I’ll never be perfect but a decent person.”

Your survival mechanisms take over.

(1/4) “I’m 51 years old. A lot happens in 51 years. I met a lot of people out here and they all agree that nobody ever saw it coming. You think, well maybe I might be homeless for a week or so and maybe even a month, at the most, but then you keep having ups and downs. It’s hard when you’re out on the streets and you get discriminated against. You get looked at funny. You have long nights because your military training tells you you’re not safe. When I’m not safe, I don’t sleep. That makes for a long day when you have to have rest but also protect yourself at the same time. Your survival mechanisms take over.”

There should have been a way out for me.

(2/4) “I just got out of high school and had lost my security guard job. My father was on my nerves about doing something with my life. At the time, my cousin and uncle were in the military. So I joined. I was a military policeman when one day my sergeant pulled a gun on me. They covered it up because he was not black, and I was. They slowed down my promotions. But, nothing happened to him. He was a CHP officer. Afterwards, he went to Desert Storm and embarrassed the entire team. He turned out to be a coward and got out of the military with dishonorable discharge. It went on his permanent record, but it didn’t matter when he went home because he was still a CHP officer. There should have been a way out for me. I should have benefits and stuff like that. But the same information in the database that was in there in 1989 is not there anymore. I was already trained to be a police officer but I didn’t go into that field, which made it hard for me to get a job. Everything I saw and witnessed really put a bad taste in my mouth about the police in general. They’re arrogant, cocky, racist, prejudice, and discriminate on all class, sex, color, religion. 75% of them are like this. That leaves 25% core, good people that protect and serve the people. The rest of them are there for themselves.”

I didn’t know I was suffering from PTSD and depression at the time. 

(3/4) “My sister lives right across the street from the golf course. She wouldn’t bring me twenty dollars if I ask. She’ll buy me something to eat, and this and that, but she’s so white washed that she’s scared of Downtown Oakland. We grew up in the same neighborhood that was a predominately black neighborhood in San Francisco, but she’s so used to being where she is now. When I lived further west, she wouldn’t come. I had no money and no food in my apartment. One time I didn’t have anything, I asked my sister to come and meet me down here. She met me at the Pak n Save at Emeryville instead because she was scared. I haven’t always been homeless, but I didn’t know I was suffering from PTSD and depression at the time. ”

I’m too young to stop. 

(4/4) “You may look like you’re going to college, but you may rarely be making ends meet with a degree. With any other place in the country, it’s not like that. It’s crazy. It doesn’t make sense. They make it very hard. But you can’t be lazy just because it’s hard. There’s this saying, ‘When the going gets tough, the tough gets going.’ Do I even look like I’m homeless? These are my walk around clothes. I went on an interview just yesterday for an assistant manager position at a grocery store. No, I’m not lazy. I’m too young to stop. ”

When you look at my transcript, you can see when I was sober and when I wasn’t. 

(1/2) “It goes back to when I was playing college football. My addiction was pain pills. Over the last thirteen years, I’ve had eight surgeries, and they’ve slowly built up over time. Oxycon, narcos, Vicodin. I’m pretty responsible when it comes to work and being on time, meeting deadlines. But during the last three or four years of it, it started affecting my jobs and school. Vicodin is the same plant as heroin so there’s a big withdrawal with it. So when I woke up every morning, I felt sicker and sicker. Towards the end, I wasn’t taking it for pain. I was taking it to feel better. I would skip work to get more Vicodin. When you look at my transcript, you can see when I was sober and when I wasn’t. When I was sober, I had all A’s. When I wasn’t, I had C’s and B’s. My mom knew I was spending more money. I paid all my bills, but all my extra money would go towards Vicodin.”

I want to be a drug and alcohol counselor.

(2/2) “I just found out this morning I got accepted into East Bay. I want to get my bachelor degree in accounting and go to Berkeley or USC to get my masters. I want to be a drug and alcohol counselor or do accounting for Cityteam. I like numbers, math, and business. I’m 35, a little older, and accounting kind of sets you apart.”

Everything I did started falling into shambles

(1/4) “I graduated from the program and am now an intern. The intern cycle is about six months. I’ve been here for about two years now. I stick around and stay residential.”

“Why did you stay?”

“I don’t know. I didn’t really have anywhere to go. I didn’t really want to go. I’m afraid to go back out there. I’m an alcoholic. I drank a lot. When I drink, I don’t stop. I didn’t really start a life for me out there. It’s made me more recluse, more of a loner. My relationships were estranged. It made me unpredictable and extreme. It drove me away from my family and made me selfish. But that’s just human stuff too. Everything I did started falling into shambles. I destroyed it. I pulled the house down on myself.”

Alcoholism is not a moral issue. It’s a physical allergy. 

(2/4) “My mom and dad are addicts as well. Their mom and dad too. It keeps going. My aunt still drinks. My dad still drinks. My mom died from drinking. I grew up in a house for three years where everybody smoked crack. I’ve always believed in God since I was a little boy. I never went to church too much, but I knew he was there. I remember looking up and cursing to him because my dad was pretty abusive when we were younger. I started smoking weed at twelve with my dad and older sister. I didn’t want to but they peer pressured me into it. It’s hilarious, but not really. You get used to it. It’s weird the stuff you can get used to. It was normal, but eventually, you can’t keep'taking it. It gets too much. So I left when I was 16 to Missouri. At 18, I moved to live with my dad in Southern California. I was working with him, and his boss was this Vietnamese guy named KC at a furniture shop. Horrible temper. This guy was so mean. My dad was already a full blown alcoholic. He was drinking every day, on the job. It was okay with the boss. ‘This is my medicine. I need it,’ he would say. I began drinking with him, daily, at work, and that was when my addiction started. Alcoholism is not a moral issue. It’s a physical allergy. My body responds differently than other people’s does to alcohol. I can’t drink casually. Some people can have a couple drinks, be okay and not want anymore. Other people can’t. When I start drinking, I can’t stop. I don’t have a limit. I can’t drink in moderation. This phenomenon of craving starts where I keep drinking and drinking.”

I understood the drinking was a big problem and it would mess up our relationship. 


(¾) “I had a relationship with a girl in Colorado. I met her when I was traveling with the carnival. She was 18, and I was a few years older. I quit right when we met, and it lasted for six months. Right when we started dating, I said right when I start drinking, it would be the end of our relationship. It was. We were together for three years, but I basically screwed it all up. I stopped after the end of our relationship. But I couldn’t stay sober. I understood the drinking was a big problem and it would mess up our relationship. I grew up with alcoholics. I understand it’s nothing good, but I still drank. I couldn’t stay sober. I was in Oregon when my mom asked me to go to a program with her. She told me I was one of the only people she trusted to go into a program with. But she was dying, and I couldn’t do anything but watch. I couldn’t go to the program with her anyways. I was still drinking and wasn’t ready. I talked to my older sister on New Years of 2015. I was crying to her, telling her about my life. My older sister goes: “You’ll never do what you want to do if you keep drinking.” It hit me. But what really hit me was that she cried. She said, “I miss my brother. I just miss my brother.” She asked me when was the last time I was sober for a year. I couldn’t remember. I was like 12 the last time I was sober for a year. I’m 29 now. I was 27 when she asked me that. I said alright, I’ll go into a program.”

I’m still learning surrender and let my issues all go to God and let him run the show. 

(4/4) “I ended up coming here on the fourth of July but got kicked out in three months. I had issues with people telling me what to do. Most people who come in do. But Marcu, an intern, at the time kept in contact with me every day. When I left, he asked me, ‘You’re coming back right?’ I was trying to think of a good reason not to come back here. I had been sober for four or five months and thought I could help a brother, Evan, who was struggling with alcoholism in Colorado. Marcu said, ‘No you need to come back here and finish what you started.’ One of my great aunts, who I’ve never met, got on the phone with me and told me, ‘If you learn one thing from that program that makes you a better man, then it’s working.’ There’s so much that come with recovery. It’s a whole different world. Recovery and addiction are two different things. I never wanted to get in it. Once I got in, I never thought I would get out. But I did. I’m still nervous. I’m still shaky. My recovery’s not solid. There are no guarantees. I’m still trying to learn how to work the program for a living. Because I’m dysfunctional to a certain degree. I don’t think the same way as other people do. I didn’t grow up with normal parents. I grew up in a broken home. I’m still learning surrender and let my issues all go to God and let him run the show. Because I’ll wreck house. I’ll mess it all up. I know it.

I’m a lot harder on myself because I don’t have the excuses. 

(1/4) “A lot of these guys who I’ve come up in recovery with grew up in families -- if they had any family at all -- where it was really rough for them. I’m a little bit different. I grew up in a stable family with decent money and all of the opportunities. I grew up in the hills of Montclair. My dad was a lawyer. I’m a lot harder on myself because I don’t have the excuses. I had every opportunity and still managed to screw things up. It was just the path God had for me. I think He puts us here in these situations to make us better people sometimes. I’m right where He wants me to be right now. It’s a weird thing.”

I’m done asking for help from people. I’ve got to do this myself. 

(2/4) “I had been through a fancy rehab before, a place called Sober Living by the Sea for rich kids down in Newport Beach. It didn’t work out. My brother had connections with people down here and said, ‘Well, you’ve had the high class one. Now you’re going to do the down-and-dirty rehab -- a little tougher on the streets of Oakland.’ It was funny because it’s a lot easier to recover if you’re living in a condo on a beach on Newport. But I felt like it was kind of a joke. I felt like it was a place where rich parents sent their kids when they didn’t want to deal with them. I don’t know how many of those kids actually recovered, to be honest with you. Probably not many of them. I grew up in Oakland, so I’m not unfamiliar with the nitty-gritty of the streets. This is actually my second time around. I had a problem with methamphetamines. That’s how I ended up here. I graduated from the program about three years ago, had a good job, went out into the world, and started getting a little bit of success. But I didn’t have my connection with God quite right so I started using again and messing around with drugs again. I also had a problem with gambling. I got down to the point where I didn’t have anything. I have a brother whom I’m very close to. I like to joke and say he’s the good brother. He’s always tried to help me in the past. I just turned 57 two days ago. I’m done asking for help from people. I’ve got to do this myself. I can’t ask for my little brother to help me anymore. I remembered Cityteam and came back down here and said, ‘Help me’. We work and serve meals everyday to the homeless. It is a little more… it gave me a close-up of how many people are out there in pain and poverty. It’s just a totally different program. A totally different way of life.”

Sometimes Cityteam is like just you and God. 

(3/4) "A few days ago on my birthday I woke up feeling a bit melancholy, and I was like, ‘God, I’m 57 and look what I got.’ I started feeling bad about it, but I turned it around. I got out and started helping people around here. There’s always somebody who needs help, whether they need clothes or something to eat. There’s always some way you can help someone here. It’s a surefire way of helping you stay sober. Have you ever read the book of Jonah? You should. It’s only like three pages. Basically, sometimes we want to run from God, but God put Jonah in a fish. Being here is like being in a fish. Sometimes Cityteam is like just you and God. Those are the kinds of things that save you. It really sucks sometimes, but it’s what saves you. You got to go through that to get saved."

I’ve got a feeling, somewhere out there, I know I have skills that can be of value to somebody.

(4/4) I’m not nervous about using drugs again. I’m a little nervous about being 57 and finding suitable employment. Most people don’t want to hire an older guy. But I trust that God’s got something in store for me and He’s not just gonna throw me to the wind. I’m pretty sure something good will happen to me. They always say, ‘Trust in God.’ I’ve always had a hard time with that, but now, I’m not too worried about it. I don’t ever expect to be rich. I had my shot at it already. At one time, I had a lot of money and owned homes, but I don’t need that to be happy anymore, really. I just want to be comfortable. As long as I have a place to live and little bit of money, I know I’ll be okay. Being a multi-millionaire… that’s not really in the future for me, and I don’t need that. Sometimes if I think about it too much, I might work myself up, but I don’t get too concerned about it. God will provide. I know that sounds kinda corny, but, that’s how I feel.
I’ve got a feeling, somewhere out there, I know I have skills that can be of value to somebody. I’m just not sure in what capacity yet. I’m just gonna keep going forward and hope something good happens. It doesn’t sound like a very sound plan at this time, but… [laughs]”
